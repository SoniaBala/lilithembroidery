Getting Started

What You'll Need

Embroidery needles: These needles have bigger eyes than sewing needles to ease the insertion of the floss.

Embroidery floss: The thread you’ll be using to embroider. Floss comes in a variety of beautiful colours.

Embroidery hoop: Hoop is made of two rings. The fabric goes in between the two rings. This helps hold your fabric taut, making it easier to embroider.

Fabric: The base for your piece. Cotton, linen and muslin work well. White fabric is a good colour to start with. Avoid stretchy fabric when you are starting out.

Scissors: Small sharp scissors are best to snip floss without fraying the thread.

Water soluble pen or pencil: To mark the design on your fabric. Water soluble pen is useful to wash out the outline of your design with cold water.

Some Basic Stitches

Running Stitch

A simple embroidery stitch that looks like a dashed outline. To start the running stitch, bring the needle to the front and punch it down a short distance away. Leave the same distance in between and bring the needle back up to the front. Continue these two steps to outline the line in your design.


Back Stitch

This stitch is perfect for outlining your embroidery designs. Start as you would for the running stitch by punching the needle down and then up at equal intervals. Move the needle back where you punched in before and bring it down. Bring the needle to the front at a little distance away from the filled in stitch. Continue until the outline is complete.


Chain Stitch

Chain stitch forms a row of linked stitches creating a bolder line. To start off, bring the needle to the front and create a loop with the thread. Punch the needle close to where you brought it up and without going all the way in, bring the needle up a short distance away. Make sure that the thread is under the needle before you bring it up.


Satin Stitch

Satin stitch is perfect for filling small areas of your design. It can be executed by repeatedly pulling the needle up along one end of your design and then punching it through along the opposite lines of the shape you are trying to fill. It's generally not preferred to use the satin stitch to fill large areas as the thread can become slack once you remove the hoop.


Stem Stitch

Stem stitch works best for both straight and curved outlines. It works similar to the back stitch. Instead of bringing the needle back to the entire length of the stitch to punch up, bring it to the halfway point of the stitch. Make sure that you are not splitting the stitch when you bring the needle up and that you stick to the same side of the stitches.


French Knot

French knots can add texture to your designs. To create a french knot, wrap the thread around the needle twice and punch the needle down close to the entry point. Keep the working thread taut but not too tight to get the perfect french knot. They are popularly used to fill the middle of flowers and to create textured fill patterns.


Feather Stitch

Feather stitch is a linked stitch (like the chain) perfect for borders and layering with other stitches to create texture. It works like an open ended chain stitch. Instead of punching down close to the point of entry, you punch down farther away to create the open end of the V. Alternate the stitch on both sides to create the moving effect of the stitch.
